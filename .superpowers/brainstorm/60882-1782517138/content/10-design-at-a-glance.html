Combat design — at a glance

The whole system assembled. Read it as one machine — if anything's off, point at it and we'll fix before I write the doc.

Core frame

Continuous BG-style overworld; snap to a soft grid when battle starts. Terrain carries into the fight: elevation, obstacles, chasms/water gaps. Renders fluid, resolves discrete.

Two-tier army

Characters (have facing; gear-driven; the wildcards) + Minions (facing-free chess pieces). Player = the "king": kill a commander and their whole army despawns.

The pieces (start with 4)

Toadstool (pawn: move cardinal / capture diagonal) · Cat (bishop) · Ent (rook) · Frog (knight-leaper, ignores terrain). Sliders blockable; Frog unblockable.

Movement = gear

Player starts at 1 cardinal step. Armor pieces add movement (boots→diagonal, leggings→knight, chest→queen-2). Grants union into hybrid pieces.

Item power budget

One budget = rarity × dropper's level, split across movement + defense + effects. Movement & defense trade off on the same item. Nothing lasts forever.

Weapons

Each has 1+ attacks targeting square patterns (spear=front, dagger=diagonal, bow=ring, fireball=3×3 AOE, flail=all-around-but-weak). Cooldowns, not spell slots.

Turn economy (per player, per round)

Character: move + one weapon action. Plus one minion: a single chess move (step or capture). Limited activations = "command" is a resource (loot can grant +1).

Many players, no fixed sides

Free-for-all: alliances, betrayal, gang-up-on-the-leader. Tactical layer is N-aware now; formal pacts + netcode are a separate design pass.

Damage matrix — two layers
Minion → Minion: binary capture (chess; level-independent; capturer survives & takes the square)
Minion → Player: damage = f(level) vs defense — the infinite axis
Player → Minion: normal weapon damage vs minion HP/def (attack type matters; no free AOE wipe)
Player → Player: weapon damage vs defense (the duel layer)

Terrain: positional rules in m-v-m (extend reach, block uphill, shove off cliffs); damage multiplier vs players (high-ground/flank/backstab).

Difficulty & progression — and the keystone

Enemy level rises with distance from spawn (raw numbers = the unbounded axis; composition/AI/terrain = texture). Your gear comes from behind the frontier, so it's always tougher ahead — never invincible, can't be cheesed by grinding low zones. Keystone: brute force gets out-scaled at the frontier, but chess captures are level-independent — so beating a stronger foe requires winning the chess match. Emergent planning is the mandatory path to victory.
